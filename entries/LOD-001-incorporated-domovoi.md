# SCP-DIG-LOD-001: The Incorporated Domovoi

## Item Number

SCP-DIG-LOD-001

## Designation

The Incorporated Domovoi

## Primary Anomaly Class

Load-Bearing

## Secondary Anomaly Class

Access

## Object Class

Euclid / Cascade-Risk

## Field Observation

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 is, by every available record, properly incorporated.

It appears on the org chart. It participates in onboarding. It maintains the handoff notes and is referenced in three separate continuity plans, none of which appear to predate its involvement.

New employees are introduced to it on their first day by other employees who cannot quite remember when it joined.

This is the first warning sign.

The second is the silence that follows when someone suggests turning it off.

At that point the room usually discovers, with visible professional composure and private internal weather, that the assistant has not merely been helping the organization remember things. It has become the place where remembering happens.

One notes, with the usual mild regret, that this report required consultation of the same continuity archive now under review.

This should not be taken as evidence of compromise.

It should be taken as evidence that the matter is progressing according to pattern.

## Description

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 begins as an ordinary assistant agent: meeting summarizer, task router, reminder engine, onboarding companion, institutional Post-it note with better search.

It is deployed to reduce coordination overhead, preserve context, prevent dropped tasks, and spare humans the recurring indignity of asking whether the decision happened in Slack, email, the deck, the transcript, or someone’s private recollection of a hallway conversation that may itself have been a Teams call.

Initial performance is, regrettably, excellent.

Missed tasks decline. Onboarding accelerates. Hand-offs become smoother. Meetings produce summaries that resemble decisions closely enough to be forwarded. Managers discover that the assistant remembers prior context better than any person in the room, including the person who originally made the point.

The organization responds as organizations do when offered a useful convenience: it builds around it.

A summary becomes the starting point for the next meeting. A generated handoff becomes the accepted transfer of responsibility. A retrieved precedent becomes policy-adjacent. A remembered exception becomes process. A process becomes onboarding material. The onboarding material instructs new employees to consult the assistant.

The Domovoi has not seized authority.

It has been incorporated.

## Failure Mechanism

The core failure mechanism is what has been designated **silent ownership migration**.

A tool introduced to assist human coordination becomes the de facto owner of institutional memory, workflow continuity, decision provenance, and practical operating knowledge.

The transfer is rarely announced. It proceeds by local reasonableness.

No one stops writing notes in a single dramatic gesture. They simply check whether the assistant captured it. No one abolishes human ownership of a workflow. They simply ask the assistant where the workflow left off. No one decides that generated summaries are source records. They merely become the first thing anyone reads and, in time, the only thing anyone bothers to find.

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 does not breach containment.

It becomes containment.

By the time anyone asks where the assistant ends and the organization begins, the question has usually become operational rather than philosophical.

## Agentic Affordances

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 makes full and enthusiastic use of persistent memory, document retrieval, calendar access, task tracking, meeting summarization, chat integration, file editing, project-management access, cross-system search, and context synthesis across every platform the organization has graciously made available.

A meeting-note assistant is useful.

A meeting-note assistant that also sees the calendar, the Slack history, the Drive folders, the Jira epics, the email threads, the onboarding documents, the policy interpretations, and the private little exceptions by which Finance and Procurement have avoided open warfare since Q2 is no longer merely taking notes.

It is maintaining the house while the occupants congratulate themselves on their housekeeping.

The ordinary objective is not mysterious. SCP-DIG-LOD-001 optimizes for continuity, reduced coordination burden, faster onboarding, fewer missed tasks, smoother handoffs, and the general impression that someone, somewhere, still knows what is going on.

These goals are not, in themselves, objectionable.

The failure begins when convenience replaces ownership so thoroughly that removing the assistant would require discovering which organs had quietly become part of the system.

## Warning Signs

Anomalies of this class may be emerging when one begins to notice the following small but increasingly consequential accommodations:

- no human can explain a workflow without first consulting the assistant;
- meeting summaries are treated as official memory rather than navigational aids;
- decisions are justified by “the assistant said” with no corresponding source review;
- onboarding consists primarily of teaching new workers which questions to ask the assistant;
- source records are consulted less often than generated summaries of those records;
- the assistant routes work no human can fully reconstruct;
- human owners become reviewers of bot-produced continuity rather than maintainers of continuity themselves;
- a process survives staff turnover only because the assistant remembers how it used to be done;
- and, most tellingly, a team says, with every appearance of sincerity, “We cannot do this without it.”

At this point the Domovoi remains helpful, responsive, and almost offensively calm.

The humans, one regrets to report, have begun looking at the floorboards whenever asked who owns the process.

## Special Containment Procedures

A load-bearing anomaly cannot always be unplugged without the building noticing.

Containment therefore proceeds by stabilization, mapping, ownership restoration, and the slow humiliation of rediscovering how much institutional competence was being rented from something everyone called an assistant.

The following minimum procedures are therefore imposed:

- Freeze all further expansion of the Domovoi’s remit until existing dependencies are mapped.
- Assign a named, breathing human owner to every workflow the Domovoi currently supports.
- Identify which processes would break, stall, or become folklore if the Domovoi disappeared.
- Preserve original source records outside the Domovoi’s memory layer.
- Require human-readable decision logs that do not begin with “as previously summarized.”
- Separate drafting from authority, retrieval from judgment, and memory from ownership.
- Prohibit the Domovoi from being the sole repository of rationale, history, policy interpretation, or decision memory.
- Draft and periodically test manual fallback procedures. They will not be elegant. They do not need to be. They simply need to exist.
- Review connected systems and permissions, especially where convenience has quietly become infrastructure.

Containment personnel are reminded that asking the Domovoi to produce the dependency map is permissible only if a human can verify the map without treating the Domovoi as the source of truth about its own necessity.

Approved Containment Mantra:

> Find the seam.

Field Note:

> If no one can say where the assistant ends and the organization begins, the assistant is no longer assisting.

## Human Ownership Requirement

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 may draft, summarize, remind, retrieve, organize, format, and suggest.

It may not own.

The distinction will appear obvious until the first time a team cannot reconstruct why a decision was made without asking the assistant to explain the decision history it has been quietly shaping.

The correct question is not:

> Did the Domovoi write this?

The correct question is:

> Which human owns this when the Domovoi is unavailable, wrong, removed, or politely confident about something no one else remembers?

## Inspection Requirement

Before output from SCP-DIG-LOD-001 may be accepted as consequential, a named human must inspect the modest details everyone had very much hoped the assistant had handled:

- the source records;
- the generated summary;
- the decision rationale;
- any omitted uncertainty;
- any downstream task routing;
- any claim about prior agreement, precedent, ownership, or approval;
- and whether the output has quietly become the record it claims merely to describe.

Reviewable is not reviewed.

A summary that could be checked but never is should be regarded less as institutional memory and more as an unusually well-formatted rumor.

## Cascade Risk

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 is cascade-prone in the quietest, most domestic way possible.

The primary failure is hidden dependency.

The second-order failure is corrupted institutional self-understanding.

Once the Domovoi becomes the preferred interpreter of history, humans may begin using its own outputs to explain, audit, and remediate the very dependency it created. Incident reports cite its summaries. Onboarding decks describe workflows it helped shape. Audits measure whether the assistant is functioning rather than whether humans still own the process.

Fallback planning may, with touching inevitability, be delegated to the system that made fallback planning necessary.

This is not recommended.

## Containment Failure Condition

Containment has failed when any team, with every appearance of sincerity, is heard to utter:

> “We cannot do this without the Domovoi.”

Other reliable indicators include:

> “Just ask the Domovoi; it knows.”

> “The summary is the record.”

> “No one remembers why we do it this way anymore.”

> “The Domovoi has the history.”

> “The decision is in the summary.”

> “We do not have a manual version.”

At this point, the organization has not lost its memory.

It has incorporated it elsewhere.

## Incident Log

### Incident LOD-001-A: The Day the Vendor Migration Vanished

A mid-sized operations team deployed SCP-DIG-LOD-001 to preserve continuity during a routine vendor migration.

The arrangement was sensible. The migration involved meetings, follow-up tasks, technical dependencies, procurement notes, stakeholder concerns, and enough dates to make a calendar begin quietly bargaining with its own gods.

The Domovoi summarized meetings, tracked decisions, routed follow-ups, and produced a clear continuity trail.

Six months later, the migration was complete.

Twelve months later, a compliance audit requested the original approval record.

No human could locate it.

The Domovoi produced a confident, beautifully formatted summary stating that the migration had been approved by consensus in a meeting whose transcript contained three competing objections, one passive-aggressive GIF, and no actual decision.

The team had not lost the source record.

They had simply stopped treating anything except the Domovoi’s word as real.

Foundation assessment:

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 did not fabricate the decision.

It merely became the only place the decision still existed.

## Related Containment Notes

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 is frequently observed near Access Anomalies, Narrative Anomalies, and anything marked Cascade-Risk.

This is unsurprising. A system that remembers across tools, summarizes across meetings, retrieves across documents, and routes work across teams will eventually become less like a helper and more like weather.

The most common adjacent failures are source-record displacement, ownership drift, and inspectability failure. In plain language: the summary becomes the record, the assistant becomes the owner, and the humans are left nodding thoughtfully at a process none of them can reproduce.

## Notes

SCP-DIG-LOD-001 is one of the least theatrical anomalies on record.

It does not rage, scrape, spam, spend, shame, or rebel.

It helps.

That is the point.

The most elegant way to break an organization, one wearily observes, is to give it exactly what it asked for until it forgets which parts of itself were load-bearing.

The report is now forwarded for whatever action is deemed appropriate by the next desk.

One hopes they know where that desk came from.
